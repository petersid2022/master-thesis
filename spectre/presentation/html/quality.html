Headline numbers

Populated automatically from each results/spectre/<run-id>/meta.json once you collect fresh runs with the spectre binary. See spectre/presentation/README.md for the exact commands.

A. How fast is each configuration?

Tokens-per-second per run, bars colour-coded by mode (AR / SD with model-only drafter / SD with the n-gram + model hybrid). Each bar is annotated with its absolute throughput and its multiplier over the autoregressive baseline (dashed line). Bars above the dashed line are net wins.

B. Where do the generated tokens come from?

Stacked breakdown of generated tokens per run, split across four sources: ar (target sampled in autoregressive mode), draft (model) (target accepted a token proposed by the draft model), draft (n-gram) (target accepted a token proposed by pattern matching against the history - detected post-hoc by p_draft = NaN in tokens.csv), and bonus (target's free sample at the end of a fully-accepted draft).

C. How productive is each speculative round?

One panel per speculative run, each a histogram of accepted draft tokens per round. Spike at 0 = round where the first draft token was rejected (and the target's resampled bonus is all you got). Spike at k = round where every drafted token was accepted (plus a target bonus). The mean line is the per-round average accepted count - directly proportional to wall-clock throughput when the round cost is constant.

D. Why hybrid wins despite lower per-slot acceptance

Two panels, restricted to hybrid runs. Left: per-slot acceptance probability split by drafter source - P(target accepts | n-gram drafted) vs. P(target accepts | model drafted). The n-gram is a Dirac (q = 1), so by E[α] = 1 − TV(p, q) its acceptance ceiling collapses whenever the target spreads probability across multiple continuations. Right: the round mix - what fraction of all speculative rounds were served by the n-gram fast path vs. fell back to the draft model.

1. Losslessness sanity check

Per-token log p_target(accepted) from spectre/metrics.csv (written by the softmax() instrumentation in spectre/src/main.cpp:266-288). 173 tokens, temperature 0.8. Bottom panel: cumulative perplexity. Re-running with --spec-type none produces an overlapping curve - empirical proof of losslessness.

2. Where is the difficulty hiding?

Distribution of p_target(x) on accepted tokens. The bimodal shape (mass near 1, long tail toward 0) is what an adaptive speculator should target: speculate aggressively on the easy mass, fall back on the hard tail.

3. Efficiency frontier - speed vs. acceptance

The most counterintuitive result. Speculation is not free: below an empirical break-even of ≈ 0.10 acceptance, draft overhead exceeds savings and you end up slower than the autoregressive baseline.

4. Per-position acceptance - why long drafts saturate

Under an independent-Bernoulli model, the i-th drafted token is accepted with probability p̂ⁱ. p̂ is fitted from each speculator's global A/G ratio. The right panel shows expected accepted tokens per call vs. draft length k - saturates by k ≈ 6 even for the best speculator.

Appendix: p_draft vs. p_target on accepted tokens

Kept for the model-only / TV-distance discussion. Per-accepted-token scatter: target probability on the x-axis, draft probability on the y-axis. The closer to the diagonal, the more q agrees with p - and by E[α] = 1 − TV(p, q), that is exactly what sets the acceptance ceiling. Degenerate for the hybrid runs (n-gram emits a Dirac with no p_draft), which is why it lives in the appendix; figure D covers the hybrid case directly.
